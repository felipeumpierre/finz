# Portfolio Allocation Guidelines

Reference for evaluating asset allocation, concentration risk, geographic diversification, and rebalancing strategies. These are guidelines, not rigid rules — always consider the individual's specific situation from their profile.

---

## Asset Distribution by Risk Profile

### Conservative (Low Risk Tolerance)
- **Equities:** 20-40%
- **Bonds/Fixed Income:** 40-60%
- **Cash/Money Market:** 10-20%
- **Alternatives (REITs, commodities):** 0-10%

Typical for: retirees, near-retirement (5-10 years), very risk-averse investors, those depending on portfolio income.

### Balanced (Moderate Risk Tolerance)
- **Equities:** 50-70%
- **Bonds/Fixed Income:** 20-35%
- **Cash/Money Market:** 5-15%
- **Alternatives:** 0-10%

Typical for: mid-career professionals, families with dual income, 10-20 year horizon.

### Aggressive (High Risk Tolerance)
- **Equities:** 70-90%
- **Bonds/Fixed Income:** 5-20%
- **Cash/Money Market:** 3-10%
- **Alternatives:** 0-10%

Typical for: young professionals, long horizon (20+ years), high income relative to expenses, strong emergency fund already in place.

### Age-Based Rule of Thumb
- Classic: equity allocation = 100 - age (a 35-year-old holds 65% equities)
- Modern adjustment: equity allocation = 110 - age (accounts for longer life expectancy and low bond yields)
- These are starting points, not prescriptions. Risk tolerance, income stability, and family situation override age-based rules.

### Family Situation Adjustments
- **Single, no dependents:** Can afford higher risk
- **Couple, dual income, no children:** Moderate-to-high risk appropriate
- **Family with children:** Reduce risk slightly, ensure emergency fund is robust (6 months)
- **Single income family:** More conservative, larger emergency fund (6+ months)
- **Nearing major expense (house purchase, education):** Reduce risk for the portion earmarked for that expense

## Concentration Risk Thresholds

### Individual Position Limits

| Threshold | Level | Action |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Single position > 5% of portfolio | MONITOR | Acceptable for core holdings, review quarterly |
| Single position > 10% of portfolio | WARNING | Consider trimming unless it is a core broad-market ETF |
| Single position > 15% of portfolio | HIGH WARNING | Active trimming recommended. Tax cost of trimming vs concentration risk |
| Single position > 20% of portfolio | CRITICAL | Trim immediately. No single stock should be 20%+ of a diversified portfolio |
| Single position > 30% of portfolio | URGENT | Major portfolio risk. Develop a trimming plan with specific timeline |

**Exception:** A single broad-market ETF (e.g., MSCI World, MSCI ACWI, FTSE All-World) at high weight is generally acceptable because it is internally diversified across hundreds or thousands of stocks. A 40% position in IWDA (MSCI World) is fundamentally different from a 40% position in a single stock.

### Sector Concentration

| Threshold | Level | Action |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Single sector > 25% of equity allocation | MONITOR | Check if driven by market-cap weighting (e.g., tech in S&P 500) |
| Single sector > 35% of equity allocation | WARNING | Review — is this intentional overweight or drift? |
| Single sector > 50% of equity allocation | CRITICAL | Diversify. Sector-specific risk is too high |

Common concern: Technology sector often represents 25-30% of broad market indices — this is market-cap weighted and generally acceptable. Flag it only if the investor ALSO holds individual tech stocks on top.

### Asset Class Concentration

| Threshold | Level | Action |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Equities > 90% (for non-aggressive profile) | WARNING | Consider adding bonds for stability |
| Bonds > 70% (for non-conservative profile) | WARNING | May be too conservative for time horizon |
| Cash > 20% (beyond emergency fund) | WARNING | Cash drag — deploy excess to investments |
| Single asset class = 100% | CRITICAL | No diversification. Add at least one other asset class |

## Geographic Diversification

### Global Market-Cap Baseline (approximate)

| Region | Market Cap Weight |
|--------|-------------------|
| United States | ~60% |
| Europe (developed) | ~15% |
| Japan | ~5% |
| Asia-Pacific (ex-Japan) | ~5% |
| Emerging Markets | ~10% |
| Other (Canada, Australia, etc.) | ~5% |

A broad MSCI World ETF provides developed-market exposure. Add MSCI EM or FTSE EM for emerging market exposure.

### Geographic Risk Flags

| Threshold | Level | Action |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Single country > 70% | WARNING | Even the US at 60% market-cap is already high — flag if significantly above market weight |
| No emerging market exposure | MONITOR | EM adds diversification but also volatility. At least 5-10% recommended for long-term portfolios |
| No international exposure (100% home market) | CRITICAL | Home bias severely limits diversification. German equities are ~3% of global market cap |
| Eurozone > 40% | MONITOR | Check if this is intentional or home bias (German investors often overweight Europe) |

### Home Bias Warning for German Investors
German equities represent approximately 3% of global market capitalization. A German investor holding 20%+ in German stocks has significant home bias. While there are valid reasons (familiarity, EUR-denominated, no currency risk), it reduces diversification benefits.

## Emergency Fund Guidelines

The emergency fund should be in cash or near-cash instruments (Tagesgeld, money market fund). It is NOT part of the investment portfolio allocation.

| Situation | Recommended Emergency Fund |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| Single, stable employment | 3 months of net expenses |
| Single, variable income (freelance) | 6 months of net expenses |
| Couple, dual income, no children | 3 months of joint net expenses |
| Couple, single income | 6 months of net expenses |
| Family with children | 6 months of net expenses (minimum) |
| Family, single income, with children | 6-9 months of net expenses |
| Nearing major life change (job switch, move) | 6+ months |

**Important:** If the emergency fund is below the minimum, building it up takes PRIORITY over all investment decisions. Do not invest new money until the emergency fund is at target.

**Where to hold it:**
- Tagesgeldkonto (daily savings account) at a bank with German deposit insurance (Einlagensicherung)
- Short-term money market ETF (e.g., Xtrackers EUR Overnight Rate Swap) — slightly better yield but not instant access
- NOT in stocks, ETFs, or any volatile instrument
- NOT in a foreign-currency account (EUR for EUR expenses)

## Rebalancing Strategies

### Tax-Efficient Rebalancing (Preferred for Buy-and-Hold)

**Method 1: Cash Flow Rebalancing (Best)**
- Direct ALL new investments (savings, dividends, bonuses) to the most underweight asset class
- No selling required, no tax events triggered
- Slowest but most tax-efficient
- Works well with regular monthly investment plans (Sparplan)

**Method 2: Threshold-Based Rebalancing**
- Only rebalance when an asset class deviates more than 5 percentage points from target
- Reduces trading frequency and tax drag
- When triggered, sell the minimum amount from overweight to bring back within band

**Method 3: Calendar-Based Rebalancing**
- Review allocation once per year (suggest December for tax-loss harvesting synergy)
- Rebalance if deviation exceeds threshold
- Simple, disciplined, predictable

### Tax Cost of Rebalancing

Before recommending any sell-to-rebalance action, calculate:

```
Position to trim: 5,000 EUR of IWDA (30% Teilfreistellung)
Embedded gain: 2,000 EUR
Taxable gain after TF: 2,000 x 0.70 = 1,400 EUR
Tax cost: 1,400 x 26.375% = 369.25 EUR

Question: Is fixing the 5% allocation deviation worth 369.25 EUR in taxes?
```

**When the tax cost is worth it:**
- Deviation is large (>10 percentage points)
- The overweight position has specific risk (single stock, sector concentration)
- Tax-loss harvesting opportunity offsets the gain
- Sparerpauschbetrag has headroom (gain would be partially or fully tax-free)

**When the tax cost is NOT worth it:**
- Small deviation (<5 percentage points)
- The position is a broad-market ETF (low specific risk)
- Cash flow rebalancing can fix it within 6-12 months
- Embedded gains are large relative to the trim amount

### Rebalancing Decision Framework

1. Is the deviation > 5 percentage points? If no, do nothing.
2. Can cash flow rebalancing fix it within 12 months? If yes, direct new money there.
3. Is there a tax-loss harvesting opportunity on the overweight side? If yes, sell (double benefit).
4. Is the Sparerpauschbetrag not fully used? If yes, realize gains tax-free.
5. Is the overweight position a single stock or sector bet? If yes, the diversification benefit likely outweighs the tax cost.
6. Otherwise, tolerate the deviation and fix with cash flow.

## Portfolio Review Frequency

| Element | Frequency | Trigger |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| Quick status check | Monthly | Ensure nothing dramatic happened |
| Full allocation review | Quarterly or semi-annually | Check deviation from targets |
| Tax efficiency check | November-December | Year-end tax optimization window |
| Fundamentals review (per holding) | Semi-annually or annually | Earnings season, annual reports |
| Full portfolio review | Annually | Comprehensive analysis, rebalancing, tax planning |
| Emergency review | As needed | Major market event, life change, large cash inflow |
